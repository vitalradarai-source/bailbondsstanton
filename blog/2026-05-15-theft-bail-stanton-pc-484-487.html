Stanton-Specific Facts You Need

Stanton theft cases hinge on a number: $950. Below that property value, the charge is misdemeanor petty theft under PC §484, with bail typically set at $500–$2,500 per the OC schedule. At or above $950, the charge becomes grand theft under PC §487 — a wobbler the DA can file as misdemeanor or felony, with bail running $10,000–$20,000 if filed as felony.

Proposition 47, passed by California voters in 2014, set the $950 threshold and reclassified most theft below that amount as misdemeanor. Before Prop 47, the threshold was $400. Defendants with old strikes and theft priors face a different calculation: PC §666 (the petty-with-prior statute) can bump petty theft into a wobbler if the defendant has prior theft convictions plus served time in custody. Stanton repeat-offender theft cases often end up as wobblers despite property value under $950.

Stanton, an unincorporated-feeling enclave in Central OC despite being incorporated, is patrolled by the Orange County Sheriff's Department from a substation on Cerritos Ave rather than a city PD. Booking happens at the substation, but defendants are typically transferred within 6–12 hours to Theo Lacy Facility (the main OC men's jail) or the Central Women's Jail in Santa Ana. The transfer window is the moment when bond posting becomes most efficient: a bond filed at the receiving facility eliminates the transfer-day administrative confusion.

One Stanton-specific practical note: the OC Sheriff substation does not accept walk-in bail posting outside of sergeant-on-duty hours. Families arriving at the substation late at night to "post bail directly" are routinely told to wait for transfer to Theo Lacy. A licensed bondsman avoids this trap by filing the bond at Theo Lacy's intake desk as soon as the defendant arrives — sometimes shaving 4–6 hours off the release timeline.

What PC §484 / §487 Actually Says

Penal Code §484 (petty theft) and §487 (grand theft) covers: Theft of property under $950 (petty) vs $950+ (grand) — wobbler felony at $950 threshold. Understanding the statute matters because the bail schedule, no-bail-hold rules, and protective-order rules all flow from how the prosecutor charges the case.

Three points families typically miss in PC §484 / §487 cases: the charging decision is not final at booking — the arresting agency's charge can change at the DA's filing review (within 48 hours under PC §825, excluding weekends and holidays). Bail schedule amounts are not ceilings — a judge at arraignment can raise bail above the schedule based on flight risk or danger, or lower it on defense motion. Protective orders can attach automatically — under PC §136.2, a Criminal Protective Order can issue at arraignment regardless of what the protected party wants, with a 100-yard stay-away clause that may bar the defendant from returning home.

Bail Cost and the 10% Premium

The bail amount and the cost to post a bond are two different numbers. The bail amount is the dollar figure the court requires to guarantee appearance — set per the Central Orange County bail schedule and subject to judicial discretion at arraignment.

The cost to a family is the 10% statutory premium under California Insurance Code §1800.4. This premium is a fee for the surety service, not money lent to the customer — it is non-refundable regardless of case outcome (acquittal, dismissal, conviction, plea). The 10% rate is set by California Department of Insurance regulation; no licensed bondsman can charge a different rate. A bondsman quoting 5% or 8% is either operating outside the law or planning to add fees later.

What the 10% pays for: the surety company's guarantee that the defendant will appear at every court date. If the defendant fails to appear, the bondsman is responsible for either producing the defendant or paying the full bail amount to the court. That risk is what the 10% premium funds.

Mistakes to Avoid in the First 24 Hours

  1. Don't wait for arraignment to act. Many families assume nothing can happen until the defendant sees a judge. Bail can be posted as soon as booking is complete — typically within 2–6 hours of arrest.
  2. Don't pay cash bail without checking with a bondsman first. Cash bail ties up the full bail amount until case resolution (often 6–18 months) and is only returned if every court date is attended.
  3. Don't sign indemnitor paperwork you haven't read. The indemnitor agreement is a binding contract. The indemnitor is on the hook for the full bail amount if the defendant fails to appear.
  4. Don't coach the defendant on what to say at booking. Phone calls from jail are recorded. Anything said about the case can be used at trial. Limit calls to logistics: bail amount, court date, who is posting bond.
  5. Don't violate a protective order, even verbally. If a PC §136.2 CPO issues at arraignment, contact through any means can result in a new charge under PC §166. The defendant must stay away even if the protected party initiates contact.

Is PC §484 / §487 a bondable offense?

Most PC §484 / §487 cases are bondable per the county schedule. Exceptions: cases with a no-bail hold from a separate active case, parole holds, immigration detainers, or specific judicial orders. Call a bondsman with the booking number to verify in real time.

Can the protective order be modified?

Yes. The protected party can request modification at arraignment or any subsequent hearing. Common modifications: change from "no contact" to "peaceful contact," removal of the residence stay-away if both parties want to reunite. The defense attorney files the request; the judge decides.

What happens if the case is dismissed?

The bail bond is exonerated and the obligation ends. The 10% premium is non-refundable per California Insurance Code §1800.4 — it was earned the moment the surety company guaranteed appearance. Any cash bail posted directly is returned by the court.

Do I have to use a bondsman in Central Orange County?

No. Families can post cash bail directly with the court or jail. The tradeoffs: cash bail ties up the full amount until case resolution; bond costs 10% but is non-refundable. For bail above $5,000, a bond is usually the financially smarter choice.
